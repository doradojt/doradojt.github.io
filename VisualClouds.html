Cloudiness




Cloudiness is essentially the representation of the percentage of water vapor that becomes trapped in the atmosphere. All air cools as it rises. As moist air rises, the cooler surrounding air begins to change the state of the water vapor from a gas to a liquid. As this is occuring, heat is taken in to the water vapor, further cooling the surrounding air. This is known as evaporative cooling. Furthermore, as the air cools and begins to reach its dew point, the environment begins to reach its saturation level, where no further evaporation takes place. As the state change of water unfolds, heat is taken from the surrounding air to perform the scientific state change. As the air reaches a saturuation level, when it is cooled to its dew point, clouds form. Clouds are possible anywhere, however we see a fairly mixed plot from our data. Normally, we would expect to see the greatest concentration toward the equator, given how warm and moist the air is in that tropical climate. However, in September, humidity in the northern hemisphere is generally higher, as surface temps and dew points are higher. Therefore, our plot is spread out more than what would be assumed to be normal.
